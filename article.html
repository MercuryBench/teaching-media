Research debt and crippling specialization

The last true polymath is said to have been Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, with countless contributions to logic, philosophy, mathematics, engineering, psychology, physics, law and various other fields. Few of us can say to have a similarly broad expertise; even more: Most researchers would label themselves not as mathematicians but as topologists, probabilists or computational scientists; not as physicists, but as experimental physicists or string theorists etc. It is believed that this is not due to humanity becoming less intelligent but due to science becoming more and more specialised: If Leibniz's endeavours are likened to climbing all mountains in the Austrian Alps, a modern scientist aspiring to be a true polymath faces a challenge similar to that of scaling every elevation higher than 500m on Earth as well as on any known celestial object in the solar system.

Lamport's expandable/collapsable proof structure

Nonlinear narratives

Textbooks and scientific articles are usually written in a linear manner: Theorems and their proofs follow lemmata, after that we get examples, concluded by exercises left to the reader. The reader can stray from the path, jump over sections and ignore proofs which he deems to tedious to follow for the moment. Many (including the author) do this with an uneasy feeling of risking "missing out" on an important idea hidden in the ignored sections.

Humans have a deep-rooted passion for playing and exploring, with Friedrisch Schiller going as far as to say "Man only plays when in the full meaning of the word he is a man, and he is only completely a man when he plays.". Many mathematical discoveries (as happened with breakthroughs in physics and engineering) only arose from someone thinking "what happens if I change this bit?". People love to tinker, to fiddle and to observe the environment's reaction. Furthermore, fun leads to passion, passion gives persistence and motivation, which in turn is necessary for the hard work needed in order to achieve anything meaningful. It makes sense to try and introduce "explorative fun" any time when teaching mathematics (be it lecturing undergrads or writing a research paper).

There is a caveat to this: Given too much explorative freedom, anyone will be overwhelmed by the amount of choices (you can't just give a high school student a licence of Matlab and tell him to "do numerics" with it). There is a sweet spot (which may be individually different) between the yoke of a very rigid textbook and unhelpful total freedom. We think that an instructive teaching medium has struck the balance if it takes the reader by the hand, gently leads him towards helpful ideas, warns him of common misunderstandings but then takes a step back and waits for the reader to choose the directions in which he wants to proceed: More rigor, more exercise, more details or more intuition (note that those categories are by no means mutually exclusive in the long term, but for someone starting to learn about a specific field they may be at first).

A second point to this is that no textbook and no article is read by a homogenous body of people but rather by experts and students, by someone just needing a specific definition or proof, or by someone who is trying to autodidactically learn about a certain subject. No two reader will have identical prerequisites or goals and thus will need different levels of detail and explanation. We have seen this fact when we talked about expandable proof structure but the gist of it applies to the structure of a whole document as well.

Textbooks typically deal with that conundrum (if they do at all) by "starring" advanced topics or giving a roadmap of which sections to read for different kinds of readers [examples]. This addresses the problem, but only partially solves it: In order to not exclude the "weakest relevant reader", the medium has to incorporate a vast amount of text which has to be skipped in whole by the "stronges relevant reader", leaving him with the feeling that the resource he is consulting mostly consists of irrelevant material with only a few gems which are hard to find (even with navigational help by the author). More often than not, this will discourage him from reading further.

A good textbook (or article) thus should be able to hide and show content depending on the expertise of the reader. Obviously this cannot happen automatically but we believe that the act of "minimizing" "irrelevant content" will help the reader to manage the content better.

Another counterdraft to a contiguous textbook might be to use a non-linear document structure. A conventional text is actually just a long scroll of paper, but a graph (or tree) structure might to the trick: A text would necessarily need a stem covering the main story with branches diverting to "building intuition", "generalization to metric spaces", "more examples and counterexamples", "applications to physics" etc. Most readers will find some of those branches, but not all of them, interesting. By presenting the material in this form, the reader does not need to skip a large number of pages, losing track of what he read and what he leapt over.
